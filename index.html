TN CTE Approved Strategy

Computer Science Foundations

An 18-Week Hybrid Pacing Calendar designed for New CTE Teachers to perfectly map to May 2025 Tennessee State Standards.

Core Structure

Combines cutting-edge Artificial Intelligence foundations with crucial web building, system terminal utilities, and physical system architecture.

  • Backbone: Code.org AI Foundations
  • Web: CS Discoveries Unit 2
  • Electronics: Tinkercad Circuits

Major TN Alignments

Direct coverage of challenging, newly emphasized career expectations that standard curricula leave out:

  • Cloud Computing Basics (8.1-8.6)
  • AI Ethics Frameworks (13.2)
  • Network Systems Defenses (14.1)

Supplements & Gaps

This layout plugs the common syllabus holes without forcing schools to buy expensive computer components or micro-controllers.

  • Circuit Gaps → Tinkercad virtual model
  • Sys Command Gaps → Lab activities
  • Material Cost → 100% Free / Browser

Week 01

IT Pathways & Course Onboarding

Introducing systems, classroom expectations, and career fields.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

1.1 Class Safety 3.1 IT Occupations 3.2 Pathways 3.3 Professionalism

Set foundational expectations for safety in computer labs. Explore various career domains in Information Technology, specifically comparing jobs in coding, system administration, cybersecurity, and emerging AI technologies.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: Welcome & Lab Safety Guidelines Introduction to workstation safety, ergonomics, and district computing rules (Std 1.1).
  • Day 2: Career Pathway Mapping Activity Students research definitions of Networking, Coding, Design, Cyber, and Data pathways (Std 3.1).
  • Day 3: Guest Speaker Video/Reflections Screen and dissect high-interest career interviews with IT practitioners.
  • Day 4: Professional Communication Basics Introduction to digital citizenship, drafting executive emails, and collaboration (Std 3.3).
  • Day 5: Week Recap & Path Quiz Baseline assessment on pathways and career profiles.
Platform Target Code.org AI Foundations (Unit 1, Lesson 1)
Key Terminology Ergonomics, Networking, Web Design, Cybersecurity, Professionalism
Assessment Tip Have students create a brief 1-slide profile of their top choice IT occupation.

First-Year Teacher Advice: Do not rush to computers on day one. Spend this week configuring Code.org classroom accounts. Build a strong collaborative classroom culture early before introducing independent lab tasks.

Week 02

Computing Systems & Hardware Components

Dissecting the system concept, input, output, processing, and storage.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

5.1 Computing Systems 5.2 Input / Output 5.3 Memory Types 6.1 Tech Terminology

Understand how a computing system coordinates hardware parts to execute programmatic logic. Compare Random Access Memory (RAM) to local solid-state storage, and classify modern sensors as input or output elements.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: The Input-Process-Output-Storage Model Diagram physical hardware systems using everyday machine examples (Std 5.1).
  • Day 2: Under the Hood: Inside a PC Case Examine motherboards, CPUs, cooling systems, RAM slots, and drive controllers (Std 5.3).
  • Day 3: Memory Battles: RAM vs. Disk Visual interactive game where students act as cache registers, RAM, and hard drives to understand latency.
  • Day 4: Sensors & Peripherals Review real-world input sensors (LiDAR, thermal, gyroscope) that drive modern computing device ecosystems (Std 5.2).
  • Day 5: Hands-on Hardware Labeling Lab Standard component identification review and memory unit conversions (KB, MB, GB, TB).
Platform Target Code.org AI Foundations (Unit 1, Lesson 2)
Key Terminology CPU, RAM, Cache, Solid State Drive (SSD), Sensor Array, Processing Cycle
Assessment Tip Use physical, decommissioned computers if available for a hands-on component identification lab.

First-Year Teacher Advice: RAM vs Storage is the most common point of confusion for students. Emphasize that RAM is short-term active workspace (like a desk surface) and Storage is long-term file cabinets.

Week 03

Electricity, Ohm's Law & Circuits

Integrating digital electronics and physical laws inside the browser.
Tinkercad Circuits

Weekly Objective & Standards

2.1 Ohm's Law 2.2 Circuit Components 2.4 Logic Gates

Bridge the physical hardware gap by examining digital electrical theory. Introduce students to current, voltage, and resistance calculations, and explore AND, OR, and NOT logical operations through circuit design simulation.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: Introduction to Circuits & V = I * R Basic electrical physics math with custom practice calculation sheets (Std 2.1).
  • Day 2: Tinkercad Virtual Lab Set-up Onboard student accounts to Tinkercad. Run basic LED, battery, and resistor test configurations.
  • Day 3: Ohm's Law Verification Challenge Students adjust virtual resistors to see brightness change. Record voltage with multimeters (Std 2.2).
  • Day 4: Digital Switches & Logic Gates Map how single physical pushbuttons correspond to logical AND / OR circuit configurations (Std 2.4).
  • Day 5: Logic Circuit Lab Report Grade interactive schematic completions using standard worksheets.
Platform Target Autodesk Tinkercad Circuits Simulator (Free)
Key Terminology Voltage (V), Current (I), Resistance (R), Logic Gate, Schematic, Breadboard
Math Component Ohm's Law calculations: Voltage = Current × Resistance.

First-Year Teacher Advice: This unit covers a major standards gap. Because virtual electrical components are free on Tinkercad, students can instantly reset parts when they accidentally "blow up" a virtual LED, removing all logistics constraints.

Week 04

Digital Information & Hexadecimal Representation

How computers encode numbers, text, images, and audio.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

9.1 Hexadecimal / Octal 9.2 Binary Encoding 9.3 Data Quality

Compare and convert base-2 (binary), base-10 (decimal), and base-16 (hexadecimal) systems. Analyze how pixels are mapped using numeric hex codes to display high-resolution RGB imagery on monitors.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: The Base-2 System Understand state representation with physical lightbulbs. Convert simple integers to binary (Std 9.2).
  • Day 2: Introduction to Hexadecimal Why base-16 is used. Map relationship between binary nibbles and individual hex characters (Std 9.1).
  • Day 3: ASCII, Unicode & Text Translation How computers convert sequences of numbers to written language structures.
  • Day 4: Color Coding & Pixel Grids Code.org interactive pixel widget tasks. Color composition analysis using hexadecimal strings (e.g., #FFFFFF).
  • Day 5: Week 4 Number Systems Quiz Assessment on conversions and character encoding.
Platform Target Code.org AI Foundations (Unit 1, Lessons 3 & 4)
Key Terminology Binary, Decimal, Hexadecimal, ASCII, RGB Values, State Representation
Math Component Arithmetic bases: base-2 and base-16 calculation conversions.

First-Year Teacher Advice: Remind students that hexadecimal is simply shorthand for binary. Converting four binary bits into one hex digit saves engineers time and reduces reading error rates.

Week 05

Web Development: HTML Structural Code

Beginning CSD Unit 2 integration to study layout paradigms.
CSD Web Design

Weekly Objective & Standards

6.1 Terminology 10.1 Web Structure 11.1 File Management

Begin a targeted 3-week web engineering unit using the Web Lab platform. Focus on core markup concepts, nesting hierarchies, and defining visual content divisions using standard tags.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: The Architecture of a Web Page Examine HTML headers, body structures, metadata, and browser layout rendering (Std 10.1).
  • Day 2: Structural Markup Tags Explore paragraph blocks <p>, headings <h1> through <h6>, and divisions.
  • Day 3: Structured Content Layouts Build functional unordered <ul> and ordered <ol> list elements on code workspaces.
  • Day 4: Visual Sourcing & Attributes Embed local images <img src="..."> with accessibility alt attributes (Std 11.1).
  • Day 5: Week 5 Basic Structure Lab Check Review structural formatting using diagnostic code files.
Platform Target Code.org CS Discoveries (Unit 2, Lessons 1 - 5)
Key Terminology HTML, Attribute, Hierarchy, Head/Body Tags, Image Embed, Alternate Text
Assessment Tip Set up strict checks to ensure students always add alt text tags to their structural image tags.

First-Year Teacher Advice: This unit plugs a major alignment gap. Students learn that HTML creates raw structural bone frameworks before they begin styling components with CSS rules next week.

Weekly Objective & Standards

12.1 Visual Styles 12.2 Media Components 15.1 Usability Layout

  • Day 5: CSS Design Implementation Lab Styling tasks to transform unformatted HTML documents.
Platform Target Code.org CS Discoveries (Unit 2, Lessons 6 - 10)
Key Terminology CSS, Rule Set, Selector, Box Model, Class Selector, Styling Declarations
Assessment Tip Grade students on selector consistency and clean file naming practices.

First-Year Teacher Advice: Draw the Box Model on the whiteboard and physically walk through the borders. Visualizing content vs. padding prevents confusion when scaling UI components.

Week 07

Website Portfolio Project Planning

Defining development cycles and planning client structures.
CSD Web Design

Weekly Objective & Standards

11.1 Directory Paths 12.2 Digital CMS 15.1 Layout Planning

Formally initiate the personal web portfolio project required by state standards. Build multi-page file directories, plan site structures with wireframes, and draft user-experience (UX) layout plans.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: Project Scope and Constraints Showcase high-quality model portfolios. Identify required content sections (Std 15.1).
  • Day 2: Site Architecture & Wireframing Sketch responsive structures on paper before writing code. Map visual navigation hierarchies.
  • Day 3: Directory Organization Rules Construct index.html, portfolio.html, contacts.html, and a centralized asset folder (Std 11.1).
  • Day 4: Creating Hyperlinks Code anchor systems <a href="..."> linking local multi-page layouts cleanly.
  • Day 5: Milestone Code Check-in Submit verified multi-page frameworks and wireframes for rubric assessment.
Platform Target Code.org Web Lab Environment
Key Terminology Portfolio, Wireframe, Asset Directory, Anchor Link, Directory Hierarchy, Relative Path
Project Milestone Completed Site Map Diagram & HTML Page Skeleton.

First-Year Teacher Advice: Require visual sketches before students write any code. It saves significant lab time because students have a clear structural plan to follow once they sit at the computer.

Week 08

Portfolio Site Development & Launch

Writing stylesheets, coding layouts, and conducting peer reviews.
CSD Web Design

Weekly Objective & Standards

10.1 Structured Layout 12.2 Portfolios 15.1 UX Optimization

Complete the web development cycle. Write stylesheets to customize layouts, integrate image assets with alternate text, and conduct quality assurance testing across multiple platforms.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: Structural Page Coding Build rich paragraphs, styled header tags, and lists within portfolio frames (Std 10.1).
  • Day 2: Layout Design Pass Connect the external CSS stylesheet. Implement color schemes and typography classes.
  • Day 3: Asset Clean Up & Optimization Compress heavy image files and ensure relative directory paths resolve correctly (Std 11.1).
  • Day 4: Peer Usability Review Swap workstations for peer-review cycles. Verify links work and that styles remain consistent across screens (Std 15.1).
  • Day 5: Portfolio Launch Day Formally publish Code.org project links. Students submit final links to the LMS grading portal.
Platform Target Code.org Web Lab Environment
Key Terminology Relative Path, Layout Consistency, User Feedback, Visual Hierarchy, QA Testing
Project Milestone Live, published Web Portfolio Project (Std 12.2).

First-Year Teacher Advice: Set up a centralized spreadsheet of all student project URLs. These live links serve as functional digital binders where students will document work throughout the remainder of the course.

Week 09

Operating Systems & Command Line Utilities

Using the terminal to navigate local folders and network layers.
Local Shell / Labs

Weekly Objective & Standards

16.1 System Utilities 16.2 Directory Structures 16.3 Batch Scripts / CMD

Transition from graphic interfaces to command shell interfaces. Learn to navigate directories, manipulate file systems, and execute core utilities like system diagnostics via terminal commands.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: Operating System Architectures Compare how OS cores manage kernel drivers and system resources (Std 16.1).
  • Day 2: File Directory Trees Explore how folders nest. Learn absolute vs. relative paths using physical diagrams (Std 16.2).
  • Day 3: Terminal Navigation Basics Open the console window. Practice directory management commands (`cd`, `dir`, `mkdir`) (Std 16.3).
  • Day 4: Network Command Utilities Learn to test device latency and inspect active network interfaces with commands like `ping` and `ipconfig`.
  • Day 5: Command Shell Navigation Test Practical assessment where students navigate virtual folders to retrieve a hidden flag.
Platform Target Windows Command Prompt (CMD) or Repl.it bash
Key Terminology Directory Tree, Relative Path, Terminal, Console, Core Commands (cd, dir, ping, ipconfig)
Assessment Tip Build a folder structure on student computers beforehand and use a "scavenger hunt" challenge where students must find a hidden code file.

First-Year Teacher Advice: Command line interface (CLI) can feel intimidating to high school students who are used to touchscreens. Remind them that system administrators use these commands to automate tasks and diagnose complex network issues.

Week 10

The Architecture of the Internet

Unpacking packets, routing protocols, TCP/IP, and name servers.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

4.1 Internet Structure 4.2 Packet Switching 4.3 DNS Protocol 6.1 Tech Terminology

Trace how digital information travels across network layers. Demystify IP addressing schemas, domain name resolution, package routing models, redundancy systems, and end-to-end data delivery.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: What is the Internet? Analyze physical infrastructure: fiber optics, deep-sea cables, and router layers (Std 4.1).
  • Day 2: Packet Routing Simulation Visual class activity modeling how large messages break into packets and route dynamically (Std 4.2).
  • Day 3: IP Addresses and DNS Lookup Explain how Domain Name Servers function as the address book of the web (Std 4.3).
  • Day 4: TCP vs. UDP Protocols Compare reliability vs. raw speed requirements for streaming video versus email file transfers.
  • Day 5: Week 10 Protocols Assessment Review standards vocabulary and protocol behaviors.
Platform Target Code.org AI Foundations (Unit 2, Lessons 1 - 3)
Key Terminology Internet Protocol (IP), Packet, Router, DNS, TCP, Redundancy, Latency
Interactive Tool Code.org Internet Simulator (built-in widget).

First-Year Teacher Advice: Run the packet routing simulation as a physical class game. Give students paper envelopes representing data packets and have them route them through classroom desks (acting as routers). It makes abstract networking concepts highly memorable.

Week 11

Networking Topologies & Physical Systems

Analyzing physical structures, client-server models, and LAN vs WAN.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

4.4 Topologies 7.1 Server Structures 7.2 System Configurations

Differentiate between physical layout topologies like star, mesh, and bus systems. Analyze hardware requirements for local (LAN) and wide-area networks (WAN), and explain the functional relationship between client workstations and servers.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: Network Topology Patterns Learn to structure Star, Mesh, Bus, and Ring topologies. Weigh cost vs redundancy tradeoffs (Std 4.4).
  • Day 2: Client-Server Paradigms Map how client requests travel to secure backend server frames (Std 7.1).
  • Day 3: Physical Networking Media Compare Ethernet, fiber, coax, and wireless frequencies. Discuss signal loss and transmission speed limits.
  • Day 4: LAN vs WAN Mapping Design a complete network floorplan map for a modern business office (Std 7.2).
  • Day 5: Week 11 Topology Review Lab Assessment of logical network designs.
Platform Target Code.org AI Foundations (Unit 2, Lessons 4 & 5)
Key Terminology Star Topology, Mesh, Bus, LAN, WAN, Client-Server Model, Transmission Media
Assessment Tip Have students diagram the physical layout of your school's computer lab network.

First-Year Teacher Advice: Modern enterprise networks rely almost entirely on physical Star or Star-Bus hybrids. Highlight that Mesh topologies, while highly resilient, are generally too expensive for standard schools to deploy.

Week 12

Cloud Infrastructure & Virtualization

Understanding service layers (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS) and system clouds.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

8.1 Cloud Architecture 8.2 Cloud Platforms 8.3 SaaS / PaaS / IaaS 8.4 Virtualization

Explore modern cloud infrastructure systems. Define how virtualization software partitions single physical servers into multiple virtual machines, and categorize common cloud services.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: What is "The Cloud"? Demystify the cloud by learning that it's simply servers in secure data centers (Std 8.1).
  • Day 2: The Service Stack Triangle Differentiate Software as a Service, Platform as a Service, and Infrastructure as a Service (Std 8.3).
  • Day 3: Virtualization Theory Explain hypervisors and virtual server setups. Highlight savings in space, heat, and physical hardware costs (Std 8.4).
  • Day 4: Public, Private & Hybrid Clouds Analyze trade-offs between hosting services publicly versus running secure local server farms.
  • Day 5: Case Study: Enterprise Solutions Analyze business scenarios and pitch cloud migration strategies (Std 8.2).
Platform Target Code.org AI Foundations (Unit 2, Lessons 6 - 8)
Key Terminology Cloud Infrastructure, SaaS, PaaS, IaaS, Hypervisor, Virtualization, Server Farm
Assessment Tip Ask students to research a common cloud tool (like Google Docs or Spotify) and classify its service level model.

First-Year Teacher Advice: This unit targets a heavy TN standard block. Keep analogies simple: IaaS is buying raw lumber, PaaS is buying pre-cut furniture parts, and SaaS is buying a finished couch.

Week 13

Data Structures & Spreadsheet Analysis

Structuring structured data, sorting records, and cleaning logs.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

17.1 Databases 17.2 Data Formatting 17.3 Data Ingestion

Explore data management workflows. Learn the differences between structured and unstructured datasets, and practice sorting, filtering, and cleaning data tables in spreadsheet platforms.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: Structured vs Unstructured Data Learn to classify structured databases, semi-structured JSON, and unstructured audio files (Std 17.1).
  • Day 2: Formatting Clean Records Learn why system inputs need standardized formats (e.g., date formats, capitalized fields) (Std 17.2).
  • Day 3: Spreadsheet Foundations Organize lists of tech assets using Google Sheets. Master column sorting and filtering rules.
  • Day 4: Data Ingestion and CSV Imports Export database metrics into CSV files, and import them into local workspaces (Std 17.3).
  • Day 5: Data Entry and Cleaning Assessment Grading lab tasks on sorting accuracy and record correction.
Platform Target Google Sheets or MS Excel Workspaces
Key Terminology Database, CSV, Structured Data, Filter, Data Ingestion, Formatting Rules
Interactive Tool Code.org Data Explorer Widget.

First-Year Teacher Advice: This unit covers essential database standards. Use pre-made, slightly messy datasets (like class rosters with missing letters) so students can experience formatting and cleaning data firsthand.

Week 14

Data Science & Machine Learning Models

Training computers to recognize patterns inside complex datasets.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

17.4 Analytics 17.5 Data Visualization 13.2 Algorithmic Design

Explore data analytics through the lens of modern Artificial Intelligence. Train machine learning classification engines, analyze testing splits, and generate clear visual reports of data trends.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: Intro to Machine Learning Differentiate traditional manual programming from machine learning training cycles (Std 13.2).
  • Day 2: AI Lab "AI for Oceans" Training Train digital classification models to identify ocean trash, and evaluate classification performance.
  • Day 3: Supervised vs Unsupervised Models Map visual examples of labeled datasets (supervised) versus clustered categorization models (unsupervised).
  • Day 4: Creating Charts and Visualizations Build scatter plots, bar charts, and trend line graphs using Google Sheets or AI Lab exports (Std 17.5).
  • Day 5: Model Design Lab Submissions Submit completed model diagnostic dashboards for grading.
Platform Target Code.org AI Lab / Google Teachable Machine
Key Terminology Supervised Learning, Model Training, Feature, Scatter Plot, Dataset Split, Classification Accuracy
Assessment Tip Students can capture Teachable Machine model training runs to document in their portfolios.

First-Year Teacher Advice: Code.org's AI widgets are highly interactive. Help students understand that models are only as good as the data they are trained on: "Garbage In, Garbage Out."

Algorithmic Ethics & Bias in AI

Unpacking model training biases and ethical design considerations.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

13.1 Social Ethics 13.2 Ethical AI Models 13.3 Copyright Laws

Critically evaluate the societal impacts of computing systems. Study training biases, privacy issues related to user profiling, algorithmic fairness, intellectual property rules, and copyright legislation.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: What is Algorithmic Bias? Study real-world scenarios where models yielded skewed predictions due to non-representative training data (Std 13.2).
  • Day 2: User Profiling & Platform Data Practices Map how social platforms harvest user footprints to train ad-targeting engines.
  • Day 3: Generative AI, Licensing & IP Laws Discuss legal challenges surrounding copyright and generative models (Std 13.3).
  • Day 4: Socratic Seminar on AI Ethics Structured student discussion arguing ethical frameworks, system transparency, and automated policing.
  • Day 5: Synthesis Essay Lab Work Draft portfolio review articles on technology's societal impacts.
Platform Target Code.org AI Foundations (Unit 3, Lessons 1 - 4)
Key Terminology Algorithmic Bias, Intellectual Property, Generative Model, Ethics Framework, Socratic Seminar
Writing Focus 1-page analytical reflection on AI safety policies.

First-Year Teacher Advice: This ethics focus aligns directly with the May 2025 standards update. Run the Socratic seminar with simple, structured ground rules: students must reference specific class readings to support their points.

Week 16

Cybersecurity & System Defense Tactics

Learning common threat types, encryption, and defense strategies.
AI Foundations

Weekly Objective & Standards

14.1 Network Threat Layers 14.2 Phishing / Engineering 14.3 Cyber Security Policies

Analyze modern cybersecurity practices. Categorize threat vectors like malware, ransomware, and social engineering, and study security controls including cryptography, multifactor authentication, and user access policies.

Suggested Daily Schedule

  • Day 1: The Cybersecurity Landscape Outline primary threat targets: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (The CIA Triad) (Std 14.1).
  • Day 2: Social Engineering & Phishing Labs Study the mechanics of modern phishing campaigns and learn standard system validation checks (Std 14.2).
  • Day 3: Cryptography Foundations Interactive Caesar Cipher practice, and mapping how symmetric vs. asymmetric keys secure Web HTTPS connections.
  • Day 4: Institutional Security Policies Discuss the importance of password requirements, multi-factor authentication (MFA), and firewalls (Std 14.3).
  • Day 5: Threat Response Case Analysis Assessment of business remediation plans following a mock data breach incident.
Platform Target Code.org AI Foundations (Unit 3, Lessons 5 - 8)
Key Terminology Malware, Phishing, CIA Triad, Encryption Key, Multi-Factor Authentication, Firewall
Assessment Tip Use free, interactive phishing identification quizzes (like Google's Jigsaw Phishing Quiz) for diagnostic checks.

First-Year Teacher Advice: Emphasize that most security breaches are caused by human error (phishing) rather than sophisticated software attacks. This highlights why digital safety awareness is crucial for every organization.
